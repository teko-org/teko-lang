Coverage matrix & max-coverage hybrid

Who closes what,
at what cost — and the one composite that closes the most

Five cliffs the bump-arena leaves open. Six candidate models. The scoring rule is strict: a verdict of mitigated_leak_to_region does not count as closed. Under that rule the maximum-coverage design is not any single model — it is a composite: a zero-cost inferred default, one narrow opt-in for the cliff the default can't touch, and unsafe as the universal floor.

2 · The max-coverage design

Why the answer is a composite, and what composes

Reading the matrix down the columns: no single model is both always-on and total. H2 covers all five but taxes every one; H1 covers four for free but structurally cannot touch C1. The maximum-coverage design stacks the cheap total-where-it-reaches default with the narrowest possible opt-in for its one gap — and a floor underneath both.

The spine is the default because purity buys always-on closure. H1 threads one inferred reachability fact — a second-class/first-class escape lattice, computed as a bounded per-function dataflow fixpoint, not a whole-program graph — through three gates: the Ref-storability gate (C2), the mem::free affine gate (C3), and the channel-send gate (C5-transfer). Because it is inferred and erased before codegen, it costs zero annotation and zero runtime, and its C2/C3 closures are the only unqualified closed cells in the whole grid. That same purity is why it cannot reclaim a cycle: closing C1 needs exactly the whole-program liveness fact — or a runtime — that H1 declines. So the spine is the floor of capability that everyone pays nothing for, and C1 is the hole it hands upward.

The opt-in exists only to fill C1. The spine leaves precisely one reclamation cliff open. Rather than upgrade the whole language to close it, the composite adds a narrow, per-declaration region-reclaimer that the programmer reaches for only when they actually build a cyclic graph, cache, or per-request web. Below, section 3 bakes off the three candidates that can play this role.

Unsafe is the universal floor. H6 sits under both: when the spine over-rejects and no opt-in fits — an FFI ring buffer, a hand-tuned compacting pool, a bespoke intrusive structure — unsafe {} gives raw C control, greppable and ASan-instrumented, walled from safe code by the Owned<T> boundary. It closes nothing soundly; it relocates the hard cases to a marked, auditable region. Every serious systems language ships this valve, and it composes with anything because it is a containment, not a model.

What composes without conflict
H1 spinedefault

Composes with everything. It is a compile-time analysis that only ever tightens what is admissible; it adds no runtime state for any other layer to collide with. Its move-check is the seed the opt-in and the floor both reuse for transfer and consumption.

A C1 opt-innarrow add-on

Composes — it lives beside the spine, not under it. A region-reclaimer (H3/H5) or a gen-ref (H4) governs only data the programmer explicitly places in it; the spine keeps proving everything else. Its metadata is confined to opted-in objects, so the zero-cost default is untouched.

H6 unsafefloor

Composes — it is orthogonal by construction. Inside the block, all safe machinery is suppressed; outside, nothing changes. The one shared obligation is the boundary check that stops a raw pointer tainting a safe field — which reuses the spine's reachability fact rather than fighting it.

What is mutually exclusive — pick at most one C1-closer
H2 islandsnot the default

H2 is the highest-coverage single model (5/5) but it is a whole second type-world — owned T ≠ T — that would sit under the language, not beside it, and permanently tax every generic and stdlib API. It competes with the spine for the foundation slot, and it violates the small-language law far harder than the composite. Adopt it and you don't also get H1's free default; you replace it. Excluded as the base, though its confined move/borrow discipline is the same idea the spine infers.

≥2 C1-closersredundant surface

H3, H4, and H5 all close (or trap) the same cliff by different runtime means — a dynamic tag, a per-deref generation, a tracing collector. Shipping two forces a three-or-four-way reference discipline on the programmer (Ref vs raw vs adopt vs Gc vs gen) that erodes the small-language win the composite exists to protect. Choose exactly one C1-closer; the section below decides which.

3 · The narrow opt-in

Three ways to close C1 — least invasive wins

The owner's criterion is explicit: least invasive and least dependent. Three opt-in mechanisms genuinely reclaim (or make safe) cyclic data. Rank them on how much new machinery they add, what unbuilt prerequisite they lean on, and how honestly they report their own limits.

Pick H3 for the composite's opt-in slot. On the owner's exact axis it dominates: it adds the least surface (one block, reusing arena machinery already on disk), leans on nothing unbuilt to function, and reports its bulk-only limit loudly. H4 fails the strict rule (it mitigates, never reclaims). H5 buys the most — individual reclaim and compaction — but at the highest invasiveness and the heaviest unbuilt dependency, and it lands a moving collector below the safety gate. H5 is the right reach only if per-object eviction inside a live region becomes a hard requirement; until then, H3's bulk drop is the least-invasive C1 closure that actually closes.

4 · The residual, re-examined

Is C1 still "irreducible"? No — it is closeable, opt-in

Before H5 and H6 were on the table, the honest verdict was that C1 — genuine cyclic reclamation — was the irreducible residual: the arena can't do it and no zero-cost static fact can either. The owner's expansion (a minimal opt-in region-GC, and unsafe mode) changes the shape of that claim.

Before the expansion
C1 is irreducible.

With only zero-cost, no-GC, no-runtime tools admissible, cyclic reclamation is provably out of reach. The spine leaks cycles to region; nothing safe closes it. It was the one cliff you simply accepted.

→
After H5 / H6 on the table
C1 is closeable — opt-in, at a bounded local cost.

With an opt-in region-reclaimer admissible, "irreducible" is the wrong word. The honest residual shrinks to: you must opt in and pay a bounded, local cost for cyclic reclamation. The zero-cost default still only leaks-to-region — that part is genuinely irreducible — but the leak is now a choice with a cheap exit, not a dead end.

The residual, stated precisely. C1 is no longer "cannot be done." It is now two nested facts, and the honesty lives in keeping them apart:

  • Irreducible: the zero-cost, zero-annotation default reclaims no cycle individually — it leaks to region and (bulk) drops. No admissible static fact changes this; it is the true price of L2 purity.
  • Reducible, opt-in: any cyclic knot the programmer chooses to place in an adopt {} region is reclaimed in bulk on drop — a bounded local cost (one tag word, two boundary compares), no GC, no whole-program pass. H5 buys individual reclaim + compaction if you need it; H6 buys full manual control as the floor.
The floor, positioned honestly

H6 unsafe {} is not a fourth C1-closer to rank against the three — it is the contained last resort beneath them. It closes C1 and C4 at C-level responsibility (a wrong free is a real UAF, caught only by ASan/PARANOID), so it is never the answer when a safe opt-in fits. Its value is that it is visible: greppable, manifest-gated, ASan-instrumented — a strict observability improvement over today's silent free-list mem::free — and walled from safe code by the Owned<T> boundary. Ship it as the floor, reach for it last.

Build status — do not oversell

Nothing here is shipping. The composite is a design; its parts sit at three very different distances from real.

The load-bearing risk is the spine. Its C2/C3 closures are only as sound as the reachability fact beneath them, and that fact is the same research-grade retrofit every capability-adding model needs. The composite's honesty is that it puts the one hard, unbuilt analysis in exactly one place — the default — and lets the opt-in and the floor stay cheap, bounded, and independently shippable.
